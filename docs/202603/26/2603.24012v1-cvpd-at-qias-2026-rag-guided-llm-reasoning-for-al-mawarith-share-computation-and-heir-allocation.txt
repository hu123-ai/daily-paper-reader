Abstract 

Islamic inheritance ( QIlm al-Maw ¯ar ¯ıth) is a multi-stage legal reasoning task: systems must identify eligible heirs, resolve blocking ( hajb ), assign fixed and residual shares, handle ‘awl / radd, and output a consistent post-taş ¯ıl distribution. The task is further complicated by variation across Sunni madh ¯ahib and national civil-law codifications, so practical models must condition on explicit legal configurations rather than a single implicit rule set. We present a retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) pipeline for this setting. The pipeline combines rule-grounded synthetic knowledge construction, hybrid retrieval (dense + BM25) with cross-encoder reranking, and deterministic schema-constrained output validation. The system achieved 0.935 MIR-E and ranked first on the official QIAS 2026 blind-test leaderboard. This result shows that retrieval-grounded and schema-aware generation is effective for high-precision Arabic legal reasoning. 

Keywords: Islamic Inheritance, Arabic NLP, Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Legal Reasoning, Structured Prediction 

## 1. Introduction 

Islamic inheritance law ( QIlm al-Maw ¯ar ¯ıth) is a high-precision, multi-stage reasoning problem: a valid solution must jointly determine eligibility, blocking (hajb ), fixed and residual shares, ‘awl / radd , and the final post-taş ¯ıl distribution. In practice, these rules vary across Sunni madh ¯ahib and civil-law codifications, so models should reason under ex-plicit legal configurations rather than undifferenti-ated parametric knowledge. QIAS 2026 (Bouchekif et al., 2025) evaluates this task with MIR-E (Mawarith Inheritance Rea-soning Evaluation), which scores both intermediate stages and final outputs, making output structure as important as legal correctness. We cast the task as retrieval-augmented struc-tured prediction and propose a three-component pipeline: (i) a deterministic rule-based gener-ator and symbolic inheritance calculator that constructs a large synthetic legal corpus with full stage-wise reasoning traces; (ii) a hybrid retriever (semantic + BM25) with cross-encoder rerank-ing for selecting the most relevant solved cases as generation context; and (iii) a constrained LLM decoding-and-parsing stage with a down-stream validation layer enforcing type, key, and cross-field invariants. This pipeline achieved a MIR-E score of 0.935 and ranked first on the QIAS 2026 blind-test leaderboard, confirming that retrieval-grounded, schema-aware generation is effective for high-precision Arabic legal reason-ing. The full pipeline is available for public use at https://github.com/swaileh/qias-mawarith-rag 1

## 2. Related Work 

The QIAS shared tasks (Bouchekif et al., 2025) established Islamic inheritance reasoning as a benchmark for Arabic legal AI, requiring both le-gal interpretation and precise multi-stage numer-ical computation. Prior work explored retrieval-augmented generation (Ahmad et al., 2025), multi-agent and fine-tuning approaches (Phuc and Văn, 2025; Mohammad, 2025), Arabic retrieval optimiza-tion (Bekhouche et al., 2025a), and encoder-based inheritance reasoning (Bekhouche et al., 2025b), alongside broader LLM benchmarking (AlDahoul and Zaki, 2025). While retrieval and ensemble methods show promise (Hamed et al., 2025; R’baiti et al., 2025), common augmentation techniques (e.g., paraphrasing, back-translation) do not en-sure legal or arithmetic correctness. Recent QIAS studies further show that even strong LLMs struggle with strict stage-wise formatting and numerical con-sistency (AlDahoul and Zaki, 2025; Ahmad et al., 2025). 3.2. Rule-Based Generator and Symbolic Calculator 

To obtain high-fidelity supervision, we adopt a 

symbolic-first data synthesis strategy. Each sam-pled heir configuration is solved by a deterministic inheritance calculator that resolves all QIAS stages in order, eligibility and blocking ( hajb ), fixed and residual share assignment, global adjustment (‘Awl / Radd), and taş ¯ıl, prior to any natural-language re-alization. This ensures every generated instance is jurisprudentially valid and arithmetically consistent by construction. Scenario generation covers spouse, descen-dants, ascendants, siblings, and extended-family categories under controllable difficulty regimes (basic , advanced , mixed ). To improve coverage of rare but evaluation-critical regimes, we addition-ally inject targeted templates for ‘Awl-heavy and Radd-heavy configurations, grandfather–sibling interactions, and classical special cases (e.g., 

Umariyyah ). A uniqueness constraint over heir combinations reduces duplication. Using this pipeline, we generated approximately 100,000 unique synthetic cases. Each symbolic solution is rendered into four com-plementary views: Arabic problem text, natural-language Q&A form, explicit reasoning trace, and full structured output. The natural-language Q&A rendering is used as the primary retrievable docu-ment for indexing.

4.1. Evaluation Protocol 

Evaluation follows the official QIAS 2026 protocol (Bouchekif et al., 2025): predictions are scored at each intermediate reasoning stage (heirs, blocked, shares, ‘Awl/Radd, final distribution) and aggre-gated into a weighted MIR-E score. Set-valued stages use order-invariant F1, while value stages use indicator scoring. Full scoring definitions and stage weights are given in Appendix A. All results are evaluated on the 200-case development split and 500-case test split; final ranking uses the offi-cial blind-test leaderboard 2.

4.2. Off-the-Shelf Benchmark 

Before full pipeline optimization, we benchmarked off-the-shelf models to select a strong open-source base. Results in Table 2 show that Qwen 3.5 9B provides the strongest initial reasoning among open models, while output completeness remains a bot-tleneck. This observation is consistent with prior QIAS studies reporting useful legal reasoning but recurring failures in structured-output reliability (Al-Dahoul and Zaki, 2025; Ahmad et al., 2025). This trade-off motivates our design choice: start from the strongest open reasoning prior, then improve structural reliability through retrieval grounding and post-generation validation. 

4.3. Official Blind-Test Ranking 

On the official QIAS 2026 blind test, the system ranked first overall. Table 3 summarizes leader-board performance. The narrow margin at the top (0.935 vs. 0.931) indicates a highly competitive setting. Our rank-1 result supports the practical ro-bustness of combining curated legal retrieval with deterministic post-generation validation. Relative to prior QIAS designs based on retrieval prompting, multi-agent orchestration, or direct fine-tuning (Ah-mad et al., 2025; Phuc and Văn, 2025; Mohammad, 2025), the main finding is that curated legal retrieval with deterministic validation improves both compet-itiveness and submission stability under MIR-E.

4.5. Error Analysis 

Despite strong aggregate performance, residual failures are concentrated in a few high-impact cate-gories: 

• Blocking logic (42%) : multi-generation hajb 

interactions remain the dominant error source. 

• Fraction arithmetic (31%) : uncommon de-nominator interactions still trigger occasional numeric drift. 

• ‘Awl handling (18%) : edge cases with tightly coupled reductions are underrepresented and difficult. 

• Formatting failures (9%) : long generations can still induce partial schema violations. These patterns are consistent with the task struc-ture: errors arise less from surface language un-derstanding and more from compositional legal-arithmetic dependencies. Future improvements should therefore prioritize explicit blocking-graph reasoning and targeted training on rare denomina-tor/‘Awl templates. 

## 5. Discussion 

Our results yield three key findings. First, strong base LLMs remain insufficient without structural support: modern instruction-tuned models produce plausible legal reasoning but still fail on strict stage-wise consistency (AlDahoul and Zaki, 2025; Ah-mad et al., 2025). Second, retrieval-grounded and schema-constrained generation provides a robust improvement path; relative to earlier QIAS pipelines (Ahmad et al., 2025; Phuc and Văn, 2025; Moham-mad, 2025; Bekhouche et al., 2025b), reliability-oriented engineering around generation is as im-portant as model choice. Third, curated PDF jurisprudential evidence consistently outperforms Web-only retrieval on semantic quality, reinforcing that domain-bounded corpora are better suited for high-precision legal reasoning (Hamed et al., 2025; R’baiti et al., 2025). Error analysis further shows that remaining bottlenecks are compositional legal-arithmetic dependencies, not surface-level Arabic understanding. While the system is competitive, several limita-tions remain: 

• Jurisprudential coverage : current rule pro-files do not yet support all major Sunni schools and civil-law variants. 

• Source sensitivity : Web evidence can re-duce semantic precision when fusion is not tightly controlled. 

• Residual reasoning errors : multi-generation 

hajb dependencies and rare denominator in-teractions remain difficult. 

• Evaluation scope : MIR-E only partially re-flects explanation faithfulness and practical us-ability. Future work will extend symbolic profiles to addi-tional madh ¯ahib , add confidence-aware retrieval with source attribution, incorporate blocking-graph constraints for neuro-symbolic error reduction, and complement MIR-E with expert juristic review. 

## 6. Conclusion 

We presented a retrieval-augmented, schema-constrained pipeline for Islamic inheritance reason-ing at QIAS 2026, combining symbolic data synthe-sis ( ∼100,000 cases), hybrid retrieval with cross-encoder reranking, and deterministic output valida-tion. The system achieved 0.935 MIR-E and ranked first on the official blind-test leaderboard, showing that progress on Arabic legal reasoning depends on explicit control of evidence quality, stage-level consistency, and schema compliance. 7. ## E. Source Ablation: Additional Analysis 

The main paper shows the headline numbers for the source ablation (Table 4). Below we break the results down further by quality levels and question difficulty. 

E.1. Results by Quality Levels 

We grouped retrievals into four bins – excellent ,

good , fair , and poor – based on the combined sim-ilarity score. The pattern was quite clear. With PDF-only, 190 out of 200 retrievals fell into the top two bins ( excellent or good ), and there were zero poor retrievals. PDF+Web was still decent – no poor cases either – but a portion of the results shifted from good down to fair . It seems like adding web documents broadens the context but dilutes the semantic precision. Web-only told a different story. Most retrievals (176) ended up in the fair category, and 23 were 

poor . Almost none reached the top bins. This confirms that noisy web sources are not reliable on their own for this kind of legal task. 

E.2. Question Difficulty 

We also looked at how difficulty affects the results. Out of the 200 development questions, 61 were simple (30.5%), 67 moderate (33.5%), 33 complex (16.5%), and 39 very complex (19.5%). The aver-age complexity score was 5.14 out of 10, so the set is reasonably diverse. What we found is that PDF-only retrieval stays strong even on harder questions, but Web-only gets worse fast as difficulty goes up. This tells us that source quality matters more than how hard the question is – at least for the retrieval stage of the pipeline. 

## F. Extended Discussion 

F.1. Key Findings in More Detail 

We highlight three main takeaways from this work. 

Off-the-shelf models are not enough. Our benchmark shows that modern LLMs can generate plausible legal reasoning on inheritance problems, but they struggle with strict stage-wise consistency. Missing fields and wrong output formats are com-mon problems. This is consistent with what other teams reported in earlier QIAS editions (AlDahoul and Zaki, 2025; Ahmad et al., 2025). The models “know” the law at some level, but they cannot re-liably produce all the required output fields in the correct format. 

Retrieval grounding helps, but engineering matters too. The gap between the top teams on the leaderboard was small (0.935 vs. 0.931). At this level, the choice of model matters less than what you build around it. Our experience suggests that careful post-processing, validation, and fall-back logic contributed as much to the final score as the retrieval itself. Compared to earlier QIAS approaches that used retrieval prompting (Ahmad et al., 2025), multi-agent systems (Phuc and Văn, 2025; Mohammad, 2025), or encoder-based meth-ods (Bekhouche et al., 2025b), the lesson is: spend-ing effort on making the outputs reliable can pay off more than switching to a bigger model. 

Curated sources beat web sources. PDF-based retrieval was consistently better than web-based retrieval across all the metrics we checked. Web sources provide slightly more keyword overlap, but their semantic quality is lower and they bring in noise. The legal-NLP community has pointed this out before (Hamed et al., 2025; R’baiti et al., 2025), and our experiments confirm it once more for Arabic inheritance law. One last observation: the errors that remain are mostly about compositional legal reasoning (blocking chains, fraction arithmetic, rare ‘Awl edge cases), not about Arabic text understanding. So the next improvements should probably come from better symbolic reasoning modules, not from better language models. 

F.2. Limitations and Directions for Future Work 

There are several things we could not address in this version of the system: 

• Coverage of legal schools : Right now the rule profiles cover the main cases, but we do not yet handle all the differences between the four Sunni madh ¯ahib . Some civil-law codifica-tions also have their own variants. Broadening this coverage is the most important next step. 

• Noisy web sources : When web evidence is included without enough filtering, it can hurt re-sults. A confidence-aware retrieval approach, where the system estimates how trustworthy each source is, could help manage this issue. 

• Hard reasoning cases : Multi-generation blocking ( hajb ) and uncommon denominator combinations are still the main sources of error. We think explicit symbolic constraints – like a blocking graph or a fraction checker – could target these specific failure patterns. 

• Evaluation beyond MIR-E : The MIR-E met-ric checks whether each stage is correct, but it does not measure how useful or under-standable the explanations are. Expert review from Islamic law scholars, together with cross-benchmark evaluation, would give a better pic-ture of real-world readiness.
