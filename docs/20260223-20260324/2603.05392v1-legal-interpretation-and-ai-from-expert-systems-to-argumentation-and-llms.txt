# Legal interpretation and AI: from 

# expert systems to argumentation 

# and LLMs *

## Václav Janeček and Giovanni Sartor 

Abstract 

AI and Law research has encountered legal interpretation in different ways, in the 

context of its evolving approaches and methodologies. Research on expert system has 

focus ed on legal knowledge engineering, with the goal of ensuring that human -

generated interpretation s can be precisely transferred into knowledge -base s, to be 

consistently applied. Research on argumentation has aim ed at representing the 

structure of interpretive arguments, as well as their dialectical interactions, to assess 

of the acceptability of interpretive claims within argumentation frameworks. Research 

on machine learning has focused on the automated generation of interpretive 

suggestions and arguments, through general and specialised language models, now 

being increasingly deployed in legal practice. 

Keywords 

Interpretation, Artificial intelligence, Expert systems , Argumentation, Large language 

models 

# 1. Introduction 

This chapter discusses how AI and law research tackles legal interpretation. We first 

introduce the concept of legal interpretation (Section 2) and then move into the ways 

in which interpretation has been addressed within different strands of AI and law 

re search: legal expert systems (Section 3), argumentation (Section 3), and finally 

machine learning and generative AI (Section 4). 

# 2. The concept of legal interpretation 

Legal interpretation is a pervasive aspect of legal practice, at all levels and for all legal 

professions: judges, lawyers, public administrators, legislators. It is also a task which 

concerns all citizens, whenever they have to figure out what the law req uires of them 

or how it can help them. A vast literature exists on legal interpretation, adopting various 

theoretical approaches, typically focusing on specific legal systems or sections of it. 

Here we shall not enter the jurisprudential debate on interpre tation, but just shortly 

introduce the concept of interpretation in order to specify the focus of this chapter.

The concept of legal interpretation, broadly understood, covers every attempt at 

determining the meaning of a legal text (most typically a statutory provision) and every 

outcome of such interpretive attempts. Importantly, legal interpretation often seeks t o

determine the meaning of a provision with a view to apply it (or not) to a specific 

scenario and eventually to act on the rule contained in the interpreted provision. 

Often , a more restrictive concept of interpretation is adopted, focusing only on those 

attempts at determining the meaning which are motivated by an initial stage of doubt. 

If there is no doubt about the precise meaning of the interpreted provision, the legal 

rule can be applied based on an automatic and unreflected “understanding” of the text, 

without the need for any interpretation . As expressed by the traditional saying in claris 

non fit interpretatio (Dascal and Wroblewski 1988). Doubts may arise from an 

unprecise formulation of the text as well as from various contextual -pragmatic 

conditions, e.g., the fact that the prima facie meaning would lead to undesirable 

consequences, or that it would lead to i mplications not fitting the plausible intentions 

of legislators or not matching important social values or widespread conditions 8. 

Consider, for instance, whether the rule “ No vehicles in the park” (Hart 1961) covers 

bicycles or scooters ridden or driven by children or adults, and what undesirable 

consequences might spring from different interpretations of that provision. 

The outcomes of interpretati ve attempts can be of two main kinds: ( a) declarative 

interpretations that ascribe to the interpreted provision the meaning that is the most 

immediate and plausible according to semantic rules and generally shared pragmatic 

presuppositions, (b) interpretations that deviate from declarative meanings by 

broadening (extensive interpretations) or narrowing (restrictive interpretations) the 

scope of a provision . Thus, going back to the above example, an interpretation of 

“vehicle” that also applies to e-scooters may be viewed as declarative, an 

interpretation that extends it to kick -scooters may look extensive (assuming vehicles 

are usually assumed to be motorised), and an interpretation that excludes e -scooters 

may look rather restrictive. Extensive interpretation comes close to analogy (Canale 

and Tuzet 2017) which however is beyond the scope of this chapter. 

# 3. Legal interpretation in expert systems 

In its initial stage (1970’s –1990’s), AI and law research focused on the so -called 

symbolic AI, based on human -generated knowledge representation. It was generally 

assumed that to develop an intelligent system, humans had to provide a formal 

representation of the relevant knowledge (usually expressed through a combination of 

rules and concepts), coupled with algorithms for making inferences out of such 

knowledge. Different logical formalisms (rule languages, classical logic, modal and 

descriptive logics, formal argumentation, etc.) and computable models for inferential 

processes (deductive, defeasible, inductive, probabilistic, case -based, etc.) were 

developed and applied. 

The typical structure of a system based on symbolic knowledge is represented in 

Figure 1. Note that humans appear both as users of the system and as creators of the 

system’s knowledge base (experts, possibly helped by knowledge engineers). 3

Figure 1. Knowledge -based system 

In the context of research on knowledge -based systems, the issue of legal 

interpretation concern s mainly the human knowledge engineer and users of the 

systems: it asked how legal sources should be modelled so that the correct legal 

interpretation is transferred into the knowledge -base of the system, to be consistently 

applied to the cases submitted to the system. 

The relation between knowledge representation and the solution of interpretive doubts 

emerged in the context of the research by Layman Allen and Charles Saxon, who 

emphasised doubts emerging from syntactic ambiguities, and the possibilities to 

rigorously express interpretive choices by injecting logical structures into legal texts ,

through logical connectives and formatting (Allen and Saxon 1995). Consider, for 

instance, the statement: “Individuals with a degree in law and in computing may apply”. 

This statement is syntactically ambiguous because it is not clear whether only 

indivi duals having both degrees may apply, or whether one of the two degrees is 

sufficient. Allen and Saxon argued that the drafter (or the knowledge -base engineer) 

should use formal representations such as: 

IF An individual has a degree in law OR 

The individual has a degree in computing 

THEN The individual may participate. 

This formulation clarifies that the one degree is sufficient, eliminating the syntactic 

ambiguity (if the interpretation is that both degrees are needed, the logical connective 

“OR ” should be substituted with an “AND ”). The analysis by Allen and Saxon showed 

that the creation of a knowledge -based system involved interpretive choices with 

regard to doubts arising from syntactic ambiguities, which should be solved in order to 

select the interpretation to be included in the knowledge base. 

Further studies also addressed interpretive doubts resulting from 

ambiguity/vagueness pertaining to the meaning of legal texts that are being modelled 

in a knowledge base. In the context of a knowledge -based system, the uncertainty 

around the meaning of the text and the terms used therein can be addressed in two 

basic ways: ( a) at the time when the system is applied to a specific case, by the officer 

tasked with that case, or ( b) at the time when the knowledge base of the system is 

prepared, by the knowledge engineer tasked with the drafting or revision of the 

knowledge base. 

In the first case the system will ask the officer whether the term to be interpreted 

applies to the case (the system may additionally support the officer by providing 

access to relevant documents such as legal sources, doctrines, etc.). In the second 

case the knowledge base is expanded with further rules and concepts meant to specify 

the chosen interpretation (Sergot and others 1986). 4

Consider, for instance, the above -mentioned interpretive doubts concerning the “No 

vehicles in the park ” provision . Assume that the knowledge -based system contains 

the following rule: 

Rule 1 : x is prohibited in the park IF x is a vehicle. 

Assume that John would like to enter the park with his device, which is a racing bike. 

To positively answer the query “Is it the case that John’s device is prohibited in the 

park?”, the system needs the information that this device is a vehicle. 

Following the first approach, the information would be provided to the system directly 

by John , who may be queried by the system: “Is it the case that your device is a 

vehicle?” If John says “YES”, th is information would be added to the system’s 

knowledge base, so that the system could conclude, by applying the Rule 1, that “ Your 

device is prohibited in the park !”. 

Following the second approach, the knowledge engineer could ex ante expand the 

system’s knowledge base with an interpretive rule such as: 

Rule 2: x is a vehicle IF x is bicycle. 

Based on this additional rule, the system will ask John : “Is it the case that your device 

is a bicycle ?”. Assume that John gives a positive answer . Then, by chaining Rule 1 

and Rule 2, the system will be able to conclude that John’s device is prohibited from 

the park. Under this second approach the task of determining whether a bicycle is a 

vehicle has been taken over by the knowledge engineer. 

The expansion of the knowledge -based system with interpretive rules (such as Rule 2 

above) raised an issue concerning the allocation of the “interpretive power”: to the 

extent that interpretive rules are embedded in the knowledge base, the task of 

interpre tation is transferred from the officer tasked of applying the rule in concrete 

situations to the knowledge engineer who builds the knowledge -based system before 

it is deployed. This more abstract (and ex -ante) approach to interpretation has been 

criticised for not being sensible to specific circumstances. For instance, it may be 

argued that while the officer tasked with applying Rule 1 could h ave easily concluded 

that the children’s bike of little Tom does not count as a vehicle, it would have been 

more difficult for her to exclude that Tom’s by is a vehicle if the knowledge engineer 

had already introduced in the knowledge base the interpretive rule stating that bikes 

are vehicles . This is because once an overbroad interpretive rule is introduced in the 

system, it becomes difficult to put that interpretive rule aside without there being an 

additional exception -granting mechanism in the AI system. 

# 4. Legal interpretation and argumentation 

The knowledge -based approach relegated interpretation to the margins of AI and law 

research, entrusting it to the users of the system or to the knowledge engineers who 

supplement the system with the additional interpretive rules. This was because a

logically consistent knowledge base —to be applied through deductive reasoning —can 

include provisions and terms that are susceptible to different interpretations , but it 

cannot include alternative incompatible interpretations of such provisions and terms. 5

And so without a robust approach for resolving interpretive inconsistencies at the 

system’s level, this task was kept within the purview of humans. 

Eventually, t his limitation of AI systems in respect of legal interpretation was overcome 

by AI & law approaches focusing on argumentation, namely on the explicit opposition 

of opposite points of view, as expressed in competing arguments. Following this 

approach a knowledge base does not need to be logically consistent, since the system 

can build and assess arguments based on conflicting premises extracted from the 

knowledge base. In the context of argumentation, interpretation can thus be modelled 

through the dialectical interaction of alternative proposals for meaning ascription, each 

based on corresponding interpretive canons. The canons being considered may 

include, for instance, interpretation from ( 1) ordinary meaning, (2) technical meaning, 

(3) contextual harmonization, (4) precedent, (5) statutory analogy, (6) a legal concept, 

(7) general principle , (8) history, (9) purpose, (10) substantive reasons, or (11) 

intention (MacCormick and Summers 1991). Methodologically, the canonical 

approaches to legal interpretation may vary a little across different jurisdictions ( Gábriš 

and Krzyžanková 2025 ), but for example the ordinary language canon (Slocum 2015) 

is recognised universally. 

Here is one formulation of the ordinary language interpretive canon: 

IF interpreting E as including ( OR excluding) C fits Ordinary Language, THEN 

E should be interpreted as including (OR excluding) C.

Note that interpretive canons, as the other conditionals in our examples, are defeasible 

conditionals (Sartor 2012): their antecedents provide a sufficient ground for endorsing 

the consequent, but only as long as there are no prevailing reasons to the cont rary. 

Such canons may be used for canonical expressions of meaning ascription, such as 

the claim that a term should be interpreted as meaning, excluding or including a certain 

content ( Walton and others 20 21 ), while the outcomes of such rules can be modelled 

through constitutive rules ascribing legal qualifications according to that content 

(Boella and others 2010). 

Let us consider now the case of little Tom, who would like to enter the park with the 

small bicycle he received for Christmas. The park guardian could stop him by arguing 

that the term “vehicle” in the rule “No vehicles in the park” includes bicycles, since , in 

ordinary language the term vehicle covers any transportation means that can carry 

one or more persons. This argument could be formalised by combining a specification 

of the ordinary language canon 

1. IF interpreting “ vehicle ” as including bicycles fits Ordinary Language, THEN 

“vehicle ” should be interpreted as including bicycles ,

with the assertion that 

2. interpreting the expression “vehicle” as including bicycles fits the Ordinary 

Language, 

to achieve the conclusion that indeed 

3. “vehicle ” should be interpreted as including bicycle s. 6

Note that premise (2) can be supported by further arguments (omitted here), e.g., by 

some factual evidence that the term “vehicle’’, as ordinarily understood, covers any 

kind of transportation means for people .

The officer’s argument can be countered by little Tom, for instance, on the basis of an 

instantiation of canonical formulation of the teleology canon (Purposiveness , see 

Canale and Tuzet 2023 ):

1. IF interpreting “vehicle” as excluding children’s bicycles fits Purposiveness ,

THEN “ vehicle ” should be interpreted as excluding children’s bicycles ,

and the assertion that 

2. interpreting the “ vehicle ” as excluding children’s bicycles fits 

Purposiveness ,

leading to the conclusion that 

3. “vehicle ” should be interpreted as excluding children’s bicycles .

Note that premise (2) of Tom’s counter -argument can be the supported by a further 

argument, based for instance on the fact that interpreting “vehicle” as excluding 

children’s bicycles fits the purposes of the law since it facilitates the enjoyment of the 

park to families and has no sig nificant negative impacts on the peaceful and safe 

enjoyment of it by others. Of course, it is an exaggeration to assume that litt le Tom 

would make this formal argument. Rather, Tom would flag the officer’s legal conclusion 

as being unfair. But the example shows that the model argument can formally justify 

the link between one’s interpretive views about a provision and the legal conc lusion 

that stems from it. 

The bridge between interpretative statements and substantive legal conclusions can 

be based on the idea that interpretative statements ground constitutive is -a rules, 

namely rules attributing classifications (Governatori and others 2010): 

IF E should be interpreted as including (excluding) C THEN [IF X is a C then X is 

(NOT) an E]. 

An instance would be the following 

If the expression “ vehicle ” should be interpreted as including bicycles THEN [IF 

Tom’s device is a bicycle THEN Tom’s device is a vehicle ].

where each conclusion (final or intermediate) is connected by lines to the premises 

supporting it (where the letter P in the linked oval denotes that this is a presumptiv e

inference). The arrows denote attacks between arguments having incompatible

Figure 2. Interpretive arguments 

The analysis of the graph can be accomplished according to a semantic for formal 

argumentation, according to which arguments are “IN” (i.e., they are exempt from 

relevant criticisms, in the context of the graph) iff they are not attacked by arguments 

that are “IN ” (for details, see Sartor 2025). According to this approach, the whole 

argument to the left is “OUT ”, since it is successfully attacked by the int erpretive 

argument to the right (the one b ased on the purpose of the rule, according to the canon 8

of Purposiveness), which is considered to be stronger. Note that in this model for 

(structured) argumentation we assume that an argument can only attack arguments 

that are not stronger than itself (see Prakken 2010 ). Note also that where a premise 

is “OUT ”, all consequences depending on that premise are also “OUT ”. Thus, 

according to the graph, the officer would fail to establish that Tom’s device is a vehicle, 

since her argument to that effect, based on ordinary language, is defeated by the 

prevailing teleologi cal argument that excludes that Tom’s device counts as a vehicle. 

Argumentation based approaches may also include arguments to the effect that one 

argument is stronger than other arguments, for instance, to the effect that teleological 

arguments usually prevail over ordinary language arguments (for a discussion, see 

Sart or 2023). In AI and law further work has been developed on argumentation -based 

analyses of interpretation adopting different formalisms, e.g., defeasible logic (Malerba 

and others 2021) or fuzzy logic (da Costa Pereira and others 2017). 

# 5. Interpretation and machine learning 

# (LLMs) 

While some attempts have been done to model legal concepts through neural 

networks (Philipps and Sartor 1999), in particular in domain s where no precise rules 

are available (Zeleznikow and Stranieri 1995, Ruggeri and others 2022), the main 

expectations and results for the use of machine learning in legal interpretation pertain 

today to generative AI and particularly to large language models. 

Generative AI (GenAI) includes a family of increasingly complex AI systems endowed 

with the ability to generate high -quality content. Here we focus on GenAI that 

generates text, although other modalities of output (such as image, video, or sound) 

are possi ble. Large Language Models (LLMs) are currently the core technological 

component of such systems. They are based on very large multi -layered (deep) neural 

networks, pre -trained on vast collections of texts. A technology primer on GenAI 

(aimed at lawyers) c an be found , for example, in : Melham 2025; Frankenreiter and 

Livermore 2025; or Surden 2024. 

The law provides a very suitable application domain for LLMs, with vast and possibly 

disruptive implications for legal professions and institutions. Generative AI has indeed 

spurred an impressive acceleration in AI & Law research and applications. LLMs hav e

become a major research focus and LLM -based systems are widely integrated into 

legal practice, where they can be used for multiple purposes, such as summarisation, 

translation, drafting, reviews of literature and cases, search and extraction of 

informati on, interactive examination of legal and factual matters (see more in Katz and 

others 2023; Devadas and Sangwan 2025; Ariai and others 2025; D’Alotto and others 

2025; Galli and Contissa 2025). As the deployment of LLMs in legal practice has just 

started it is still early to assess their impact on legal practices , though we may venture 

to say that their massive deployment will be relevant for legal semiotics ( Wagner and 

Marusek 2023 ), by affecting the way in which we approach different determination of 

meani ng, as they take place in different legally relevant activities, such in legal 

translation , the analysis of factual descriptions (e.g., expert opinions) and the 

interpretation of legal sources. 9

Progress has been rapid, as LLMs grow in size, achieve human -like or better 

performance on tasks requiring reasoning skills and other cognitive capacities, such 

as the ability to process multimedia content. It appears that they have passed both the 

Turing test and a growing range of qualifying exams for professional lawyers. To be 

sure, LLMs are not the only technology that powers GenAI tools, but it is the LLM 

component that has unlocked the current AI & Law boom, including a new wave of 

thinking about AI -assisted legal interpretation. 

For instance, the user can paste a legal text (e.g., a contractual clause, a statutory 

provision, or a judicial pronouncement) into the chat window and ask the chatbot to, 

for example, (1) segment provisions into operative phrases; (2) generate alternative 

paraphrases; (3) list or discuss ambiguities; (4) map provision s within their normative 

environment (other laws in the same matter, definitions, sections, recitals/purposes); 

and (5) articulate competing interpretive arguments tied to recognized canons of 

interpretation. Such outputs —the answers of the chatbot —would be useful as th ey will 

in many cases semantically resemble what a competent person would say in response 

to those interpretive tasks. Here is an example from a real conversation with a GenAI 

chatbot (ChatGPT 5.2 Thinking) about the “No vehicles in the park” rule .10

As can be seen, the chatbot’s output in Figure 3 appears immediately useful, and its 

generation only required a very simple prompt. Still, it has important limitations. It is 

(so far) problematic to say that GenAI tools, including chatbots, would systemati cally 

ground their answers in factual “knowledge”, although various techniques can guide 

the LLM to provide factually better answers and to pragmatically overcome this 

limitation (e.g., Retrieval Augmented Generation which supplements the user query 

(promp t) with links to pertinent sources). It is also problematic to say that GenAI tools 

would “reason” in the relevant sense, although various techniques can leverage 

argumentative and planning strategies to pragmatically overcome this limitation (e.g., 

CoT (C hain -of -Thought) which generates chains of reasoning steps supporting the 

presented claims). And it is problematic to say that GenAI tools would ground their 

answers in some inner “understanding” of the user prompt, although it may be argue d

that GenAI pra gmatically manipulates the approximated “meaning” of the text via the 

embedded representation of vector tokens. 

Importantly, LLM’s “competence” is primarily linguistic and discursive, not knowledge -

based or rules -based. This is what sets it apart from the previous wave of AI & Law 

techniques. As such, the current limits of the LLMs in the legal domain should not be 

underestimated: they have no sense of truth, no direct access to the physical, human, 

and social world; their ability to engage in reasoning and argumentation is limited and 

brittle; their internal working is opaque, and their explanations are often super ficial or 

misleading; they may provide false or unverified information (called “hallucinations”). 

Unthoughtful use of LLMs may induce legal decision -makers to adopt suggestions 

based on false facts, fallacious reasoning, or disregard of the human interest and the 

moral and legal principles at stake. It may deskill legal professionals, who may 

passively endorse the machine’s outputs rather than submit them to rigorous factual, 

legal, and logical checks, and who may fail to complement such outcomes with their 

own creativity. It may lead citizens to act upon mistaken legal indications and promote 

views (possibly outdated or overruled) which happen to be well represented in the 

training data, rather than views that are correct by any applicable legal standards. 

This does not exclude that a jurist can profitably entrust an LLM with legal tasks and 

specifically request an LLM to elicit interpretive issues and suggest how to address 

them. The LLM can, for example, quickly re -express a provision in different language 

registers (plain language, technical paraphrase), surface implicit structures 

(conditions, exceptions, definitions, cross -references), and propose candidate 

readings (extensive/restrictive) together with reasoning patterns and the textual cues 

that might support them. The jurist, however, should not assume that the LLM shares 

the cognitive skills that are deployed by humans in interpretation, that it possesses a 

critical understanding of legal and social facts, norms and values, and may accordingly 

apply i nterpretive canons. The LLM rather relies on the linguistic patterns it has 

learned from the large corpus of training data on which it has been trained (which may 

or may not include, only or mostly, good representations of canonical approaches to 

interpret ation). Given an input prompt (the user question), the LLM has the capacity to 

use such patterns to generate a continuation that is statistically likely (it thus captures 

legal, social and human realities only to the extent that they are reflected in linguistic 

patterns ).12 

# The importance of data and prompts 

For real -life interpretation tasks, the interpreter needs to be sure the AI system has 

access to relevant data. In this regard, it is important to note a key distinction between 

specialised GenAI tools for lawyers and GenAI tools which are publicly availab le, 

including those embedded in popular web search engines. The specialised systems 

would typically utilise domain -specific data contained in some external knowledge 

base curated by the system providers. 

This is particularly important because much of the relevant legal data is not contained 

in the documents used in the pre -training the LLM (legal sources, cases, or doctrinal 

papers and books accessible to the system at the time of the pre -training) and nei ther 

is it freely available on the Internet. Some relevant data will thus not be in the pre -

training dataset behind the LLM, and neither will it be possible for LLM -based tools to 

retrieve relevant legal authorities from the web in the post -training stage. For textbook 

examples like “No vehicles in the park”, this data limitation might not pose a particular 

difficulty, but interpretive questions about highly technical (or very recent) pieces of 

legislation could see the interpreter (here an AI system) to “h allucinate” an inexistent 

authority because a reliance on some authority could be the linguistic pattern (schema) 

learned by the LLM for this kind of interpretive task. 

The specialised GenAI tools for lawyers typically seek to plug this data/knowledge gap 

by RAG (Retrieval Augmented Generation). RAG is an architecture for optimizing the 

performance of an artificial intelligence (AI) model by connecting it with an external 

knowledge base. The data in the external knowledge base is retrieved and 

automatically added to the user query (prompt), meaning that the LLM would then 

“see” all the relevant information as part of the prompt and the generated answer could 

thus refer spe cifically to the accurate information provided. 

As should be clear by now, the quality of the GenAI answer (the generated output) is 

in large part dependent on the quality of a user prompt. RAG is one example of a 

prompt enhancement technique. A system prompt is another technique. The quality of 

the out put can also be enhanced by user -led prompting techniques, whereby the user 

ensures their input contains a full set of relevant variables that will help steer the model 

answer into the right direction (Meincke and others 2025). Various studies develop 

prom pting techniques for legal tasks specifically (Yu and others 2022, Müller -Peltzer 

and others 2025) and private vendors include many more in their standardised prompt 

libraries for their paying customers. Importantly, this ability of the user to somehow 

inf luence what GenAI “sees” and “does” via strategically crafted prompts has 

motivated a series of experiments in what is now called “generative” legal 

interpretation. 

# Generative interpretation 

Generative interpretation is a term coined by Hoffman and Arbel (2024 ) as part of their 

proposal to use LLMs as a practical aid for contract interpretation . They argue that 

courts -- instead of relying mainly on dictionaries and canons (textualism) or open -

ended testimony and context (contextualism) -- can use LLMs to find what the parties 

meant at the time of contracting by analysing the contract’s language and relevant 

extrinsic evidence in a disciplined, replicable way. The techniques and tools deployed 13 

for such generative interpretation are existing publicly available LLMs and structured 

prompts. 

The justification for this approach is that LLMs can process large amounts of text and 

are sensitive to linguistic context. As such, LLMs can help courts estimate ordinary 

meaning “in context” of other relevant documents used by the parties to the contract .

LLMs can help quantify how ambiguous a term is, fill contractual gaps, and even 

evaluate the marginal probative value of particular pieces of extrinsic evidence —while 

keeping costs down and reducing the ad hoc feel of current court practice in contract 

interpretation cases. See also Arbel 2026; Arbel 2025; Calvert 2025. Generative 

interpretation can be seen as part of a broader empirical turn in jurisprudence 

(Bystranowski and others 2025) , in the attempt to naturalise it (Lee 2024 ).

A similar idea has been around since around 2020, when scholars discussed how 

computer -assisted methods could be used to ascertain “ordinary meaning” for the 

purposes of textual interpretation of legal sources, including statutes. At that time, the 

focus w as on the methods of corpus linguistics (Lee and Mouritsen 2018; Tobia 2020; 

Nyarko and Sanga 2022), while now the same idea is tested via GenAI tools based 

on LLMs (Engel and McAdams 2024). Judges have been experimenting with the 

generative interpretation approach in their work too (Miller 2025), with some scholars 

noting a particular advantage of this approach for US constitutional interpretation (a 

textualist approach: Coan and Surden 2025) or statutory simplification (Hariri and Ho 

2026). There are atte mpts to justify the use of generative interpretation by comparing 

this method’s outputs with reliance on dictionaries and corpus linguistics (Johnson 

2025). 

Because of the LLMs’ linguistic competence, it is not easy to draw a hard line between 

generative interpretation stricto sensu and other examples of legal tasks that require 

LLMs to interpret legal documents. This concerns, for example, summarisation of legal 

documents (Dal Pont and others 2023, Deroy and others 2025, Santosh and others 

2024, Benedetto and others 2025, Zin and ot hers 2025) , assessment of the quality of 

legal documents (Boi et al 2025), or classifications of legal interpretation methods in 

existing legal document by using LLMs (Dugac and Altwicker 2025). The technical 

steps involved in the production of these outputs will be the same, given that GenAI is 

a general -purpose technology. Vast opportunities exist for the use of LLMs for the 

purpos e of knowledge extraction, which may include the translation of legal sources 

into knowledge bases, to be processed according to logic or argumentation (Dal Pont 

and others 2026). Some projects exist that aim at training LLMs in being proficient in 

legal a rgumentation, by prompting them to present arguments that comply with legal 

methods, e.g., with interpretation canons. 

The ability of LLMs to competently process natural language input and generate 

seemingly competent language output has also led to a renowned interest in Agentic 

AI systems, including multi -agent systems that have access to (and can autonomously 

control) a range of other software tools, including rules -based and knowledge -based 

tools. This makes the field of enquiry even more complex, and it is not always easy to 

say whether an interpretive task has been carried out by an LLM as such. Rather, it is 

more pre cise today to conceptualise these tasks as LLM -enabled interpretations and 

to explore precisely the methodical and social legitimacy of specific LLM -enabled use 

cases, taking into account the back -end architecture of the AI system and the data it 

utilises. 14 

# Evaluation of outputs 

A specific problem of LLMs in the legal domain pertains to the evaluation of their 

outputs. No precisely defined ground -truth (factual data) exists for checking, for 

instance, whether the LLM’s answer about the “No vehicles in the park” rule is correct 

or incorrect, justified or unjustified. The performance of LLM -enabled tasks in law is 

thus measured by benchmarks (Guha and others 2025; Chalkidis and others 2022). 

Benchmarking is about how well an LLM performs on a legal task X, whereby “well” 

may be asses sed by a human -led jury and compared to human -produced outputs for 

the same task. In interpretive tasks, such benchmarks can be formed by authoritative 

precedent decisions that confirm (or deny) certain interpretations of the source 

document, but it could also be a human -led scoring based on how persuasive the 

interpretive outputs (and supporting arguments) are for a human jury (e.g., Garzo and 

Palumbo 2025). Legal analysis tasks are thus usually assessed using qualitative 

benchmarks (Choi and Schwarcz 2025 ), rather than quantitative ones. 

LLMs generative powers can be leveraged for other kinds of tasks, some of which may 

be utilised in the automated workflow that is motivated by an interpretive task. For 

example, LLMs can be used to automate annotation of documents (Libal and others 

2026), retrieve and generate legislative definitions (Zilli and others 2025), or detect 

unfair clauses in terms of service (Panarelli and others 2026). In such tasks, their 

performance may be measured by traditional quantitative metrics, but it may come at 

the co st of reproducibility and consistency of the answers, which are notorious pain 

points of the non -deterministically behaving GenAI systems (Cohen -Sasson 2025, 

Floridi 2025a). 

Among the works on evaluating and benchmarking the results obtained by LLMs, see, 

e.g., Fei and others 2024, Tan and others 2024, Chlapanis and others 2024, Vals 

2025. 

# Critiques of LLM -enabled interpretation 

The key normative -practical question concerning the use of LLMs in legal 

interpretation pertains to the extent to which they can play a useful role, contributing 

not only to efficiency but also to the quality of legal analysis. A number of criticisms 

raise d against the use of LLMs are relevant in this regard. 

It has been argued that the GenAI advantages (such as significant time savings in 

contract review, Martin and others 2024) may be offset by the professional imperative 

to verify any LLM -generated output (Yuvaraj 2025), as stated by many professional 

guidel ines. Such controls are necessitated by reported rates of hallucinations (17 –

34% on benchmarked legal queries according to Magesh and others 2025). 

Unfortunately , hallucinations are an intrinsic feature of GenAI systems and cannot be 

eliminated by LLMs alo ne (Karpowicz 2025), though they can be limited and 

controlled, e.g., by requiring the GenAI tool to look on the internet for all the sources 

it cites. 

The cost of mitigating LLMs’ hallucination in law is high (Schepers and others 2025) 

and better -performing LLMs may not solve the verification -value tension in the context 

of the law. It has been argued that legal interpretation tasks require valid (not be tter or 15 

worse) performance (Hildebrandt 2025), though human interpretation may also fail to 

meet correctness standards. 

While RAG may be seen as a technique to mitigate hallucinations and anchor 

generated text in good legal sources, it has important limitations. The GenAI tool 

needs to know which documents to retrieve, and for this it needs to decide which 

documents/chunks are relevant for the task. The relevance is based on a compressed 

external knowledge database and the retrieval process thus presents a trade -off 

between speed, relevance, and completeness of retrieved parts of the database 

(Ferraris and others 2024; Wigge rs and Harz 2024; Weller and others 2025). Further 

research is needed to establish in what contexts and to what extent RAG may 

effectively reduce hallucinations. 

A cognate point of attack is consistency: it has been observed that on the one hand 

LLMs may provide different responses given the same prompt, and on the other hand 

that their responses may be different from the responses given by humans. It has also 

been observed that such inconsistencies have distinct effects: LLMs inconsistency has 

a larger impact on foreseeability, while human inconsistency has a larger impact on 

fairness (Cohen -Sasson 2025). Others too highlight the unreliability of LLMs in high -

stake s application areas such as legal interpretation and call the generated insights 

“artificial intuition”, rather than empirical analysis of any meaning (Lee and Egbert 

2024). 

It has been observed that statistic -based technologies such as LLMs fail to engage 

with legal sources at a jurisprudential (normative) level of analysis and instead only 

process the legal data as facts, limiting their ability to produce jurisprudential ins ights 

that would be readily informative for a participant in the legal system, as opposed to 

insight that would be informative for an external observer of the system (Janeček 

2023). It has also been observed that they fail to deliver the ordinary meaning o f

legislative sources, namely what an ordinary reader would think the source means 

(Chen 2025). This is related to the fact that they do not share our understanding of 

natural language. Cognate themes can be found in the literature focusing on distant 

read ing and distant writing (Floridi 2025b), which well captures the tensions between 

the ability to read and the ability to write, when conceived as a data -driven ability. 

The (un)reliability of LLMs in interpretive tasks has been assessed through various 

empirical tests. For example, studies show that LLMs incur in significant failures when 

extracting principles of law (Molinari and others 2025); detecting unfair clauses in 

terms of service (Panarelli and others 2025); reasoning with facts and statutes (Blair -

Stanek and others 2023), including if they try to follow the IRAC structure (Linna and 

Linna 2025). 

Some critics have pointed to possible misuses of LLMs in legal interpretation. For 

instance, Grimmelmann and others 2026 observed that the “[t]he superficial fluency of 

LLM -generated text conceals fundamental gaps between what these models are 

currently ca pable of and what legal interpretation requires to be methodologically and 

socially legitimate.” According to these authors, generative interpretation methods are 

not demonstrably reliable (reproducible and consistent) and they have an epistemic 

gap (it is not clear that what they do is what we think they do). Waldon and others 

2025 have argued that legal decision makers should not take interpretive suggestions 

of LLMs as authoritative indications of correct interpretation, but rather as 16 

“brainstorming” tools, to expand the set of interpretive suggestions and arguments that 

the judge might consider. 

In light of the criticisms just presented we may conclude that LLMs should never be 

used as authoritative oracles providing reliable legal advice on interpretation, even 

when their interpretive proposals are accompanied by apparently sound legal 

arguments. However, their ability to organise, present, and contextualise language -

embedded knowledge can support their use as companions for skilled legal 

interpreters: LLMs may synthesise at least some doctrinal debates on the interpretive 

issues at stake, and sug gest possible interpretations as well as indicate reasons for 

and against their acceptance. The lawyers using such companions, however, should 

maintain direct contact with the original legal sources, continue to use their trained 

expertise in figuring and solving interpretive issues, and carefully check the 

correctness of all arguments and references proposed by the LLMs. They should have 

the final decision, and full responsibility, on what interpretation to endorse based on 

what arguments : they should not accept an interpretation just because it was proposed 

by an LLM; acceptance should be grounded on a normative justification they endorse 

(though the LLM may support them in finding such a justification). 

This is not an optional recommendation that would be based on a comparative 

strength of human interpreters relative to LLMs. Rather, it is a recommendation based 

on LLMs’ cognitive and technical inability to discharge these significant legal tasks 

autonomo usly. It is also why it seems to us that the delegation to LLMs interpretive 

tasks in the context of agentic AI should be considered with an extreme caution, since 

in such a context the LLMs would take action based on the interpretation it has 

adopted, wit hout direct human control. Such an approach would directly incur all the 

criticisms we have just presented. Little Tom would probably object to being fined by 

some LLM -enabled system since the LLM inside the machine has autonomously 

(without a human -led va lidation) interpreted the “No vehicles in the park” rule as 

including children’s bikes. 

This brings us back to the initial criticism that the use of LLMs in legal interpretation 

may not be justified under a cost -benefit analysis, as the cost of checking the LLMs 

suggestions may outweigh the benefit of having such suggestions. Whether this is the 

case depends on the nature of the interpretive problems being considered (e.g., an 

LLM is likely to be most useful where a vast doctrinal debate exists and where that 

debate is well articulated in digitised literary sources). It also depends on the tra ditional 

information tools already available to the lawyer (a textbook or commentary may 

provide lawyers with all the information they need) and on the lawyer’s capacity to 

question the LLM to extract information that is relevant to the interpretive tasks. We 

may however venture to suggest that as the LLMs’ competence and “learned” 

knowledge base grow, so would their deployment in legal tasks. Currently, the 

reasonable expectation is that the use of LLMs in support of legal interpretation tasks 

would become less error -prone, for example through an additional layer of automated 

techniques (e.g., post -hoc legal citation verifications) or through smart practical 

implementations into robust workflows (e.g., LLM -enabled detection of inconsistencies 

or interpretiv e mistakes in a source document). This development could make 

verification less costly or it could side -step the verification -value paradox entirely. 17 

# 6. Conclusion 

The times have not yet arrived where little Tom could rely on an AI system to know 

whether after all he can enter the park with his small bike, on the basis of the system’s 

interpretation of the “No vehicles in the park” rule. The human cognitive skills required 

for legal interpretation are not currently possessed b y AI tools, and it is unlikely that 

they could be obtained through the incremental development of existing technologies. 

However, there is ample space for the deployment of AI as supporting tools for lawyers 

engaged in interpretive reasoning. In this vein, AI’s interpretive suggestions and 

arguments should be viewed as additional information provided to a competent lawyer 

ra ther than as independently reliable legal opinions. Effort should be put in supporting 

lawyers who wish to use LLMs for the purpose of legal interpretation. At present, an 

appropriate methodical guidance on how to use AI systems (rather a guidance on how 

not to use them) is missing. 

Interesting lines of research pertain to integrating approaches based on logic and 

argumentation with LLMs. One possibility consists in using the power of LLMs for 

scalable extraction of information from legal documents or documents that evidence 

legally r elevant behaviours and the translation of the extracted information into 

computable formalisms, in order to obtain a knowledge base that can support logical 

reasoning and argumentation (including in addressing interpretive issues). Another 

possibility pert ains to teaching LLMs to address interpretive issues, by prompting or 

training them to correctly apply interpretive canons and legal theories. A further 

possibility pertains to controlling the interpretive outcomes by LLMs by relying on logic, 

formal argum entation, and other standards for legal reasoning (such controls may also 

be in part delegated to other LLMs systems, prompted -trained to execute such 

controls). And yet another possibility is in (re)designing the lawyer’s workflows such 

that the benefits of different AI systems can be fully leveraged and that the risks 

discussed in this chapter are properly mitigated.
