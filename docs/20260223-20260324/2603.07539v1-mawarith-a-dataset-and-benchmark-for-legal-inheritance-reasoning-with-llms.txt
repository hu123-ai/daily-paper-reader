Abstract 

Islamic inheritance law ( ʿilm al-mawārīth )is challenging for large language mod-els because solving inheritance cases re-quires complex, structured multi-step rea-soning and the correct application of juris-tic rules to compute heirs’ shares. We in-troduce MAWARITH , a large-scale anno-tated dataset of 12 ,500 Arabic inheritance cases to train and evaluate the full rea-soning chain: (i) identifying eligible heirs, 

(ii) applying blocking ( ḥajb ) and alloca-tion rules, and (iii) computing exact inheri-tance shares. Unlike prior datasets that re-strict inheritance case solving to multiple-choice questions, MAWARITH supports the full reasoning chain and provides step-by-step solutions, including intermediate legal decisions and justifications based on classical juristic sources and established inheritance rules, as well as exact share calculations. To evaluate models beyond final-answer accuracy, we propose MIR-E 

(Mawarith Inheritance Reasoning Evalua-tion), a weighted multi-stage metric that scores key reasoning stages and captures error propagation across the pipeline. We evaluate five LLMs in a zero-shot setting. Gemini-2.5-flash achieves about 90 % MIR-E on both validation and test, while Fanar-C, Fanar-Sadiq, LLaMA 3, and Qwen 3 re-main below 50 %. Our error analysis iden-tifies recurring failure patterns, including scenario misinterpretation, errors in heir identification, errors in share allocation, and missing or incorrect application of key inheritance rules such as ʿawl and radd .The MAWARITH dataset is publicly avail-able at https://github.com/bouchekif/ inheritance_evaluation .

1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have recently achieved strong performance across a wide range of natural language processing tasks, including question answering, summarization, and legal text understanding. Despite these advances, their ability to perform structured, rule-based reasoning remains limited and is still insufficiently evaluated. For some tasks, fluent generation and general knowledge are not sufficient; models must follow precise rea-soning steps, where early errors can propagate and invalidate subsequent decisions. This lim-itation has motivated the development of mod-els designed to solve complex tasks by break-ing them into smaller steps and producing in-termediate reasoning traces before generating a final output. Recent efforts in this direc-tion include reasoning-oriented large language models such as the latest versions of Gem-ini, GPT, DeepSeek, Fanar, and Qwen, which aim to improve multi-step reasoning by decom-posing the problem into smaller steps, gener-ating intermediate steps, verifying intermedi-ate results, and correcting errors when needed. The evaluation of these models has largely fo-cused on mathematical and synthetic reason-ing benchmarks (e.g., GSM8K, MATH, and BBH; (Cobbe et al., 2021b; Hendrycks et al., 2021a; Suzgun et al., 2022)), while structured domain reasoning remains less covered, despite dedicated efforts in law (Chalkidis et al., 2022; Niklaus et al., 2023; Guha et al., 2023) and medicine (Pal et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2024). Islamic inheritance law ( ʿilm al-mawārīth ) of-fers a particularly challenging and well-defined testbed for evaluating such reasoning capabil-ities. Solving inheritance problems in this do-main requires multi-step legal reasoning, a cor-rect solution must first identify eligible heirs, then apply the juristic rules governing exclu-sion and blocking, and finally compute the ex-act fractional shares. The process is further complicated by adjustment cases such as ʿawl 

and radd .

In this work, we introduce a large-scale benchmark corpus of 12,500 Islamic inheri-tance problems, designed to support training and evaluation models for multi-step legal– numerical reasoning under strict jurispruden-tial rules. Each case is annotated with de-tailed, human-like reasoning that mirrors the step-by-step process followed by jurists: in-cluding heir identification, blocking analysis, and systematic share computation. The cor-pus covers a wide range of inheritance scenar-ios with varying levels of complexity. To en-able fine-grained evaluation, we adopt a multi-stage evaluation framework that assesses not only the correctness of the final numerical shares, but also the validity of the intermedi-ate reasoning steps involved in reaching the solution. This evaluation strategy allows for a precise analysis of reasoning errors and fail-ure modes across the core stages of inheritance problem solving. The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 reviews the related work. Section 3 provides an overview of Islamic in-heritance law. Section 4 describes the dataset used in this study. Section 5 details the evalu-ation metrics, and Section 6 outlines the exper-imental setup. Section 7 presents an in-depth error analysis. Finally, Section 8 concludes the paper and discusses potential directions for fu-ture research. 

2 Related Work 

Large language models have recently been applied to a wide range of Islamic knowl-edge tasks, including Quranic question an-swering (Bhatia et al., 2026), knowledge re-trieval (Mubarak et al., 2025; Xuan Phuc and Đặng Văn, 2025), and the analysis of hallucina-tions in Islamic content (Mubarak et al., 2025). These studies show that LLMs perform well on knowledge retrieval and basic understand-ing when answers rely on direct textual match-ing. However, they often hallucinate and show clear limitations on tasks that require struc-tured reasoning or deep domain knowledge. In (Bouchekif et al., 2025b), the authors re-port that several models, such as LLaMA and ALLaM, frequently cite non-existent Quranic verses or fabricate Hadith references. As a re-sult, the generated conclusions are not only incorrect, but are also supported by false re-ligious evidence. This behavior raises serious concerns for religious and legal applications, where correctness depends not only on the fi-nal answer but also on the authenticity and reliability of the cited sources. To mitigate hallucinations, recent work has explored Retrieval-Augmented Genera-tion (RAG) approaches. While RAG im-proves access to relevant information and en-hances factual faithfulness and citation accu-racy (Noureldien et al., 2025; Alowaidi, 2025), it remains insufficient for answering ques-tions that require multi-step reasoning. This limitation has motivated the development of reasoning-oriented models that explicitly sup-port multi-step inference. In this context, re-cent research has increasingly focused on such models that aim to move beyond surface-level text generation and promote more reliable rea-soning. Models such o3 , GPT-5 (Singh et al., 2025), Gemini-2.5 (Anil et al., 2023), Gemini3 ,

DeepSeek-R1 (DeepSeek AI, 2024), along with open models such as Fanar-C-2-27B , Falcon-H1R (Abbas et al., 2025) and Qwen3 (Shen et al., 2025) illustrate this trend by promoting more consistent multi-step inference through instruction tuning and large-scale pretrain-ing. Evaluations of reasoning-oriented lan-guage models have largely focused on math-ematical and logical benchmarks, on which these models have achieved strong results, par-ticularly in arithmetic reasoning, symbolic ma-nipulation, and competition-style mathemat-ics (Cobbe et al., 2021a; Hendrycks et al., 2021b; Wei et al., 2023). Beyond mathemat-ical and logical benchmarks, recent work has begun to investigate LLM reasoning in legally grounded settings by evaluating models on legal benchmarks such as BRIEFME (Woo et al., 2025), which require structured argu-mentation and rule-based reasoning. The authors show that GPT-4o can outperform human annotators on argument summariza-tion by producing clear and coherent sum-maries. Even within the Islamic domain, in-heritance law has received growing attention as a challenging testbed for LLM reasoning (AlDahoul and Zaki, 2025; R’baiti et al., 2025; Farouk Zaki, 2025). A similar MCQs bench-mark is MirathQA (Almasoud et al., 2026), built from 1394 inheritance cases. In contrast, the present study requires models to perform end-to-end inher-itance reasoning, explicitly generating inter-mediate reasoning steps, applying jurispruden-tial rules, and computing the final inheritance shares. 

3 Background: Islamic Inheritance Law 

Resolving an Islamic inheritance case (mawārīth ) requires a well-defined, multi-step procedure that combines juristic rules with precise fractional calculations. It begins with identifying all potential heirs mentioned in the estate description. Eligibility is then assessed: eligible heirs receive their prescribed shares, while others are excluded according to the rules of blocking (ḥajb). Two adjustment mechanisms may apply thereafter: • Radd (redistribution) applies when the to-tal of the prescribed shares assigned to the heirs is less than the whole estate and no residuary heir is present. In this situa-tion, after all fixed shares have been dis-tributed, a portion of the estate remains unassigned. This remaining portion is then redistributed proportionally among the eligible fixed-share heirs, according to their original shares. For example, if a mother receives 1/6 of the estate and a daughter receives 1/2 , the total assigned share is 1/6 + 1/2 = 2/3 . Since 2/3 is less than the full estate, the remaining 1/3 is redistributed among the mother and the daughter through radd .• ʿAwl (proportional reduction) applies when the total of the prescribed shares ex-ceeds the whole estate . In this case, the ini-tially assigned shares cannot all be satis-fied in full. To resolve this, all prescribed shares are proportionally reduced so that their sum becomes exactly equal to the es-tate. For instance, if the prescribed shares add up to 1/2 + 1/6 + 2/3 = 8/6 , which is greater than 1, each share is scaled down proportionally through ʿawl so that the total distribution fits within the estate. A correct solution requires accurate identifica-tion of heirs, strict adherence to juristic rules, and precise numerical computation. Errors at any intermediate stage propagate deterministi-cally and invalidate the final distribution, mak-ing mawārīth a suitable domain for evaluating structured, multi-step, and rule-based reason-ing. 

4 Data Description 

We construct a dataset of 12 ,500 Islamic in-heritance problems written in Arabic, fol-lowing the opinion of the majority opinion (al-jumhūr ). Each problem is written as a natural-language inheritance scenario that de-scribes the deceased and the surviving rela-tives. The corpus covers the full set of kin-ship relations defined in classical Islamic in-heritance law, including direct ascendants and descendants, siblings, spouses, and extended agnatic relatives across multiple generations (e.g., nephews, cousins, uncles, and grandpar-ents). The <think> section contains a step-by-step procedure that describes the reasoning process followed by experts in Islamic inheritance com-putation. It includes: (i) the extraction of po-tential heirs from the textual description, (ii) 

the determination of eligibility through block-ing rules (ḥajb), (iii) the assignment of pre-scribed shares, (iv) the application of adjust-ment mechanisms such as radd or ʿawl when applicable, and (v) the computation of the fi-nal inheritance distribution. Appendix B provides an illustrative example showing how a case is solved in <think> and summarized in <answer> . During the heir-identification stage, the <think> section justi-fies the inclusion (or exclusion) of each candi-date heir by applying blocking rules (ḥajb) and may cite relevant Qur’anic evidence when in-voking a prescribed-share rule.Then, for each eligible heir, it explains why a specific fraction is assigned and how this fraction depends on the presence or absence of other heirs. The rea-soning also makes the numerical procedure ex-plicit: it derives the base denominator ( taʾṣīl ), distributes the residue to the ʿaṣaba (resid-uary heirs), applies correction when needed (taṣḥīḥ ), and reports final shares as normalized fractions and percentages. Finally, it performs a consistency check by summing all allocated shares to verify that the total equals the full estate ( i.e. , the shares add up to 1). The <answer> section then presents the final result in a brief, practical summary format: it reports only the essential final shares assigned to each heir, without repeating the intermedi-ate legal reasoning and numerical steps given in <think> .For model evaluation, we use a structured for-mat that exposes the outputs of the main rea-soning stages: (i) the list of heirs who actu-ally inherit, (ii) the list of present but blocked heirs, (iii) the initial shares before any adjust-ment, (iv) the type of global adjustment ap-plied ( ʿawl or radd ), (v) the final normalized distribution (taṣīl). Appendix C details the structured output schema of our data and pro-vides a case-general template.

5 Evaluation Metric: MIR-E 

Prior work evaluates reasoning models using either final-answer scoring or LLM-as-a-judge evaluation. However, both strategies have lim-itations for multi-step reasoning tasks. LLM-based judging can be sensitive to prompt de-sign and difficult to reproduce consistently. Final-answer scoring, in contrast, reduces the entire reasoning process to a set of final values and therefore cannot distinguish between qual-itatively different failure modes—for example, a model that makes an early error (e.g., in heir eligibility or blocking) and a model that per-forms the legal reasoning correctly but makes a numerical error only at the final calculation stage. To address these limitations, we introduce 

MIR-E (Mawarith Inheritance Reasoning Evaluation), a fine-grained metric designed to assess not only the correctness of the final numerical allocation, but also the validity of intermediate legal reasoning steps. For each inheritance case, models are required to pro-duce outputs in a predefined structured for-mat (as shown in Appendix C), enabling de-tailed comparison with a gold reference solu-tion. Concretely, this format provides struc-tured intermediate outputs for the main rea-soning stages, including: (i) the list of effec-tive (inheriting) heirs, (ii) the list of present but blocked heirs, (iii) the initial prescribed shares before any adjustment, (iv) the type of global adjustment ( ʿawl or radd ), (v) the post-adjustment state, and (vi) the final normalized distribution ( taʾṣīl ). MIR-E decomposes inheritance reasoning into five components, each capturing a distinct source of potential error.

6 Experiments and Results 

This section describes the experimental setup, evaluated models, and the results obtained on the MAWARITH dataset. 

6.1 Experimental Setup 

We evaluate six LLMs in a zero-shot set-ting using Arabic prompts, without any task-specific fine-tuning. The prompt specifies the inheritance scenario, the required rea-soning steps, and the target output schema, and it enforces adherence to the formal rules of Islamic inheritance law, enabling a fair and controlled comparison across mod-els. The evaluated models include Fa-nar 3 (an Arabic-centric LLM, evaluated in both its general-purpose variant, Fanar-C-2-27B , and its Islamic-specialized variant, 

Fanar-Sadiq ), open-source multilingual mod-els (LLaMA 3 70B 4 and GPT-OSS 120B 5), a multilingual reasoning model (Qwen3-32B 6), and a commercial model (Gemini-2.5-flash). Model sizes range from 27B to over 120B pa-rameters; however, the exact parameter count of Gemini is not publicly disclosed. We ob-tained predictions via the Fanar API for both Fanar models and the Groq API 7 for LLaMA and GPT-OSS. For Qwen3-32B, we ran in-ference locally on a server with three GPUs (144GB total memory). Across all models, we use a context window of 4096 tokens and set the decoding temperature to 0.1. 6.2 Overall Performance and Reasoning Analysis 

Table 2 reports the overall MIR-E scores ob-tained by all evaluated models, including their performance across the main reasoning steps (heir identification, share assignment, and fi-nal distribution accuracy) on both the valida-tion and test sets. A similar performance pattern is observed on both the validation and test sets, with Gem-ini clearly outperforming the other models and achieving a MIR-E score close to 90%. In contrast, the open-weight models obtain overall MIR-E scores below 45%. Among them, Qwen3-32B achieves the strongest per-formance, followed by Fanar-Sadiq. Although Qwen3-32B, Fanar-Sadiq, and GPT-OSS-120B are capable of generating multi-step reasoning, they often make early-stage errors (e.g., incorrect heir identification or blocking decisions), which then propagate and inval-idate the final allocation. This behavior is expected in inheritance reasoning, where the steps are strongly interdependent: each heir’s share depends on the presence or absence of other heirs, their assigned shares, and the cor-rect application of arithmetic and normaliza-tion rules. As a result, intermediate mistakes can cascade into a fully incorrect solution. We observe that, except for Gemini, the models struggle to correctly identify eligi-ble heirs and exclude blocked heirs: Gemini achieves a success rate of 78.2%, whereas all other models remain below 25% at this stage. It also appears that errors propagate: early mistakes significantly reduce the number of cases that can succeed at later stages. All open-weight models experience their largest degradation between Step1 and Step2, high-lighting share assignment as the main bot-tleneck under fixed heirs (e.g. , Qwen3-32B drops from 24.4% to 11.8%). This perfor-mance gap may be partially explained by dif-ferences in model scale and training data . Gem-ini benefits from a larger scale and proprietary training resources. Model scale and training data diversity likely contribute to more reli-able multi-step reasoning under structured le-gal constraints. 

7 Analysis of Errors Across Reasoning Stages 

To better understand model weaknesses across the inheritance reasoning pipeline, we perform a fine-grained, stage-level error analysis. Our findings show that errors extend beyond the application of Islamic inheritance rules to ear-lier linguistic parsing steps. Indeed, all models exhibit linguistic parsing errors when extract-ing heirs from Arabic problem statements. For example, in the case: ܺ ``܇׍ﺕ ﻭޟ޻ﻙ: ﺃڐݮ੪੎ ݈ٵسٵ൘ഽ ﻭᅋᄸ ﺃڐݮﺍﺕ ݈ٵسٵ׍ﺕ ﻭﺃﺏ ﺍটগﺏ ﻭﺃﺭؑכ ৓౼׍ﺕ ﺍ੪੕ ﺍ੪੕. ܇׍ ڷݮ ܷؔၣထ ׻ཛྷ ﻭﺍﺭﺙ؟'' , some models fail to correctly interpret the compound kinship expression ``ﺃﺭؑכ ৓౼׍ﺕ ﺍ੪੕ ''੪੕ﺍ (i.e. , four daughters of a son’s son). ×4 plus ''੪੕ﺍ`` ), effectively splitting one heir into two and introducing spurious heirs. Moreover, models sometimes modify the heir type names even when explicitly instructed to preserve them exactly as provided. For instance, the model may replace ``ﺃﺏ ﺍটগﺏ'' with the equiva-lent term ''ٔ਎ﺍߓ`` , despite the prompt explicitly requiring the model to keep the original heir names without introducing synonyms or alter-native expressions. This shows that LLMs of-ten rephrase the heirs, which can bias the eval-uation if it relies on exact string matching and does not account for semantic equivalence. We observe that this error type is relatively rare for Gemini, yet frequent in the other mod-els. (iii) Count Mis-match Errors (ME): the model predicts wrong heir counts due to misreading Arabic expressions in the question (e.g., numbers and plural forms), by adding or reducing the num-ber of heirs, which then affects the shares as-signed to the other heirs.

7.3 Adjustment Errors 

We evaluate the adjustment step only on in-stances where blocking decisions and share as-signment are correct ( i.e. , B=1 and Ss=1 ). This restriction is important because the ad-justment ( ʿawl/radd ) outcome is determined by the assigned fractions and their sum: ʿawl 

applies when the total exceeds 1, while radd 

applies when the total is below 1 and no residuary heir ( ʿaṣabah ) absorbs the remain-der. If heirs are incorrectly blocked/unblocked or shares are incorrect (wrong fractions, miss-ing/extra heirs), the adjustment decision is not interpretable. The table 5 shows that Gemini-2.5-flash makes only one error out of 373 adjustment-evaluable instances (0.27%). Most other models make only a few adjustment mistakes (3.85–8.33%), whereas GPT-OSS-120B exhibits a higher er-ror rate (23.08%). This means that, once ear-lier steps are correct, the adjustment step is generally reliable for most models. A typical adjustment error is a confusion be-tween remainder handling and radd . When the sum of fixed shares is below 1, the remain-der should be absorbed by a residuary heir (taṣīl) if one exists; otherwise, it should be redistributed through radd rules. In several cases, models label these instances as radd (or omit the adjustment step) without checking for a residuary heir, which results in an incor-rect adjustment decision even when the fixed fractions are otherwise correct. 7.4 Final Allocation Errors 

This category captures computational failures rather than lack of knowledge of mawārīth 

rules. As shown in Figure 2 , such errors are relatively rare. They mainly occur at the last stage of the pipeline, final allocation . In many cases, models correctly identify that an heir should receive გႮ ૯૔۹ﺍ ᇖᆰؑ׍ (the remainder), but fail to materialize this remainder into ex-plicit numeric final shares. In some cases, models miscompute the remaining share af-ter allocating the fixed fractions and/or mis-convert it into percentages, leading to incor-rect final allocations. It is important to note that in our dataset, ʿawl and radd require recomputing the heirs’ shares. However, in our evaluation we only assess whether a case requires adjustment and which type applies (ʿawl or radd ). This strategy is adopted be-cause most instances in our corpus are simple cases without adjustment, where an “adjusted-shares” output is not applicable; scoring ad-justed shares in such cases would introduce in-consistencies in the evaluation metric. More-over, when adjustment is required, the ad-justed shares largely coincide with the final al-location, so any error in the recomputed shares would directly propagate to the final allocation step. We also observe a less common error where models report group-level shares rather than per-individual allocations (e.g., one value for “three brothers” rather than one value per brother). 

8 Conclusion 

This paper introduced MAWARITH , adataset and benchmark for evaluating legal reasoning in Arabic Islamic inheritance law. 

MAWARITH is designed to support reason-ing training by breaking down complex inheri-tance queries into explicit, step-by-step reason-ing stages. Each stage is explained and justi-fied with relevant Islamic legal texts, and re-quires accurate jurisprudential knowledge and strict rule application: (i) identifying eligi-ble heirs and blocking relations, (ii) assigning fixed shares and residuary portions, and (iii) 

producing a final distribution. To support fine-grained and interpretable evaluation, we pro-pose MIR-E , a metric that breaks down perfor-mance into step-level subscores aligned with the inheritance reasoning pipeline, enabling fine-grained error analysis. Our evaluation highlights a clear gap between commercial and open-weight models. Al-though some models can generate convincing explanations, they often fail at early structural steps (e.g., missing or hallucinated heirs, incor-rect blocking). These early errors propagate to later steps, leading to inconsistent share as-signments and invalid final distributions. We hypothesize that a primary cause is limited exposure to inheritance-law knowledge during training, since most training data is generic and insufficient to solve inheritance problems end-to-end. We expect MAWARITH to be an important resource for building reasoning and Islamic-domain LLMs that can solve in-heritance cases with detailed, step-by-step rea-soning. Future work will explore reinforcement learn-ing approaches, including Process Reward Models, to better align model reasoning with legal constraints. By providing step-level feed-back, these methods can guide models toward valid reasoning trajectories and reduce error propagation in multi-step legal reasoning. We will also extend the corpus to cover more complex remaining inheritance cases, includ-ing pregnancy-related cases, multiple deaths, the missing person ( mafqūd ), and the intersex heir ( khunthā ).

A Appendix: Heir Categories and Kinship Coverage 

Islamic inheritance law defines a rich and struc-tured set of potential heirs, reflecting varying degrees of kinship with the deceased. In our dataset, we explicitly cover all 36 legally rec-ognized heir categories as defined in classical Islamic inheritance law. These categories span a wide spectrum of kinship relations, ranging from close family members to more distant rel-atives. The dataset includes close relatives heirs who are directly related to the deceased, such as parents, spouses, and children. These close relatives typically appear with high frequency across inheritance cases and constitute the core of most inheritance configurations. They include ascendants ( e.g. , father, mother, and grandparents), descendants (e.g., sons, daugh-ters, and grandchildren), as well as spouses. In addition to these direct relatives, collat-eral relatives are also considered, including sib-lings and half-siblings (full, paternal, or ma-ternal). Under Islamic inheritance law, these heirs become legally relevant in many scenar-ios, particularly when direct ascendants or de-scendants are absent, and their entitlement is governed by well-defined blocking and priori-tization rules. For analysis, we group the 37 

heir categories into 8 kinship levels , ordered by proximity to the deceased (Level 1: closest rel-atives; Level 8: most distant extended-family relatives); see Figure 4. As shown in the figure, heirs from Levels 1–3 are the most frequent in the corpus, whereas distant heirs from higher levels occur much less often (Levels 6–8). More generally, Islamic inheritance law rec-ognizes that all legally defined heirs may be-come entitled to inherit depending on the absence of closer relatives. Accordingly, ex-tended relatives across multiple generational levels—such as nephews, uncles, cousins, and their descendants—play a legally significant role in inheritance cases where nearer heirs are absent. In such situations, inheritance is deter-mined by hierarchical succession rules, making these cases particularly suitable for evaluating long-range dependency reasoning and complex blocking mechanisms. </answer> 65 C Structured Solution Format 

To support deterministic and fine-grained evaluation, each model output includes, in addition to the free-form <think> and the concise <answer> , a structured field 

answer_structured . This field provides machine-readable intermediate results that mirror the standard inheritance workflow used by legal experts and can be parsed automati-cally. 

General schema. For each instance, 

answer_structured is a JSON object with the following components: • heirs : the list of heirs who actually in-herit (eligible heirs), each with heir (Ara-bic label) and count (multiplicity). • blocked : the list of present but excluded heirs (blocked by hajb ), each with heir 

and count .• shares : the initial allocation before 

any global adjustment (ʿawl or radd ). Each entry specifies heir , count , and a 

fraction string. • awl_or_radd : a categorical flag indicat-ing whether a global adjustment is ap-plied: "none" / "awl" / "radd" (in the dataset: জগ / עݮﻝ / ﺭﺩ ). • awl_stage : the state of the prob-lem after adjustment (used only when 

awl_or_radd is "awl" or "radd" ). It in-cludes asl_after_awl and an adjusted 

distribution .• post_tasil : the final normalized distri-bution ( taṣīl ). It contains total_shares 

(final denominator) and per-head fi-nal results via per_head_shares and 

per_head_percent .

Conventions. Heir names must follow the benchmark taxonomy (Arabic labels). Counts must be explicit whenever an heir type ap-pears multiple times. Fractions are repre-sented as strings (e.g., "1/6" , "2/3" , "3/24" ). When no adjustment is needed, awl_or_radd 

is "none" , asl_after_awl is null , and 

awl_stage.distribution may be empty. This structured representation makes in-heritance reasoning measurable at multiple levels: eligibility and blocking decisions (heirs /blocked ), initial share assignment (shares ), correct handling of global adjust-ment ( awl_or_radd and awl_stage ), and the final normalized distribution ( post_tasil ).
